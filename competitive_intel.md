# Competitive Intel Extractor — Smart Notification Center
**Reviews analyzed:** 6 competitor reviews
**Analyzed:** 2026-03-18

---

## GAPS WE CAN FILL (top 3)

**1. Notification rules by type and assignment context**
Users can't filter notifications based on relevance to them — assigned tasks, direct mentions, overdue items. The competitor offers only an all-or-nothing toggle.
> *"There's no way to say 'only notify me if this task is assigned to me or overdue.' It's all or nothing."* — Review 2
**Severity: High** — Users are actively turning off notifications and missing critical updates as a direct result.

**2. Smart daily digest that surfaces actions, not events**
The competitor's digest is a raw chronological dump. Users explicitly want prioritization — 3 things that need action today, not 47 updates.
> *"I need it to tell me: here are the 3 things that need your action today. Not 47 updates."* — Review 3
**Severity: High** — This gap was also the core finding in user interviews. Two independent sources confirm it.

**3. Reliable mobile push notifications**
Mobile delivery is inconsistent enough that users are missing deadlines they expected to be notified about.
> *"I've missed deadlines because I thought I'd be notified and wasn't."* — Review 5
**Severity: High** — A missed deadline caused by a missed notification is a trust-destroying product failure.

---

## FEATURES USERS LOVE (that we don't have)

**1. Mention notifications that actually work**
Users consistently single out @mention notifications as the one thing the competitor gets right — high signal, always relevant, always read.
> *"The mention notifications are excellent."* — Review 6
> *"The only notifications I read are direct mentions."* — Review 1
**Should we build it:** Yes, and lead with it. This is the baseline users expect. If we can extend this logic — make all notifications feel as relevant as a mention — that's the product differentiator.

**2. Notification snooze**
Users are leaving notifications unread as a workaround to remember them later. The absence of snooze is creating its own noise problem (cluttered unread count).
> *"I leave things unread as a hack to remember them."* — Review 4
**Should we build it:** Yes. Low effort, high trust signal. Snooze communicates: "we know you're busy, we'll come back." Reduces the fear of missing something if users do clear their inbox.

## THE ONE THING THEY DO BETTER

**Mention notifications.** Two separate reviewers — at 3 stars and 4 stars — explicitly praised them while criticizing everything else. The competitor has correctly identified that direct relevance (someone addressed me) is the highest-signal notification type and built that well. Everything around it is broken, but this one thing earns trust. We need to match or beat this before we can credibly claim we do notifications better.

---

## STRATEGIC RECOMMENDATION

The competitor's core failure is treating all notifications as equal — users are drowning in ambient noise and have lost trust in the system entirely. Our highest-leverage move next quarter is to launch with notification rules by type (mentions, assignments, overdue) combined with a smart daily digest that surfaces the top 3 action items — not a list of events. Both gaps are validated by competitor reviews *and* our own user interviews, making this the lowest-risk, highest-signal feature bet we can make.
