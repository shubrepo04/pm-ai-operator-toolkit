# Interview Synthesizer — Smart Notification Center
**Interviews:** Sarah (Marketing Manager), James (Engineering Lead), Priya (Product Manager)
**Synthesized:** 2026-03-18

---

## TOP FRUSTRATION

Users don't trust notifications to signal what actually matters — so they've opted out entirely or check on fixed schedules, creating their own workarounds.

This isn't a volume problem. It's a **signal-to-noise problem**. Everything looks equally urgent, so users treat everything as equally ignorable.

**Moments that prove it:**

- *"Everything looks the same whether someone liked my comment or there's a critical bug. I've trained myself to ignore all of it."* — Sarah
- *"The in-app notifications are basically decorative."* — James
- *"I turned off email notifications 6 months ago — I was getting 40 emails a day and none required action."* — Sarah

---

## SURPRISING FINDING

**Users don't want more notifications — they've already solved for fewer.** The team probably assumes users want better delivery (bell icon, email digest). But all three users have already built coping systems that bypass the notification layer entirely: Sarah killed email alerts, James checks on a rigid schedule, Priya relies on meetings to catch up.

Adding a bell icon to a system users have learned to distrust won't change behavior. The product assumption that "users need to see notifications" is wrong — users need notifications to *earn attention* by demonstrating urgency.

Shown in: all three interviews.

---

## WHAT USERS ACTUALLY WANT

Not a notification center. A **daily triage service** that does their prioritization for them.

The underlying need: *"Tell me the 3 things that actually require my action today, ranked by urgency — before I even open the tool."*

Users aren't asking for a better list. They're asking to be relieved of the cognitive load of reading and evaluating every item on the list. James articulated it most precisely: the product should tell them what to **do**, not just what **happened**.

---

## EXACT USER LANGUAGE

These are verbatim phrases suitable for product copy, onboarding, and internal messaging:

1. *"I've trained myself to ignore all of it."* — Sarah
2. *"The in-app notifications are basically decorative."* — James
3. *"Tell me what I need to DO, not just what happened."* — James
4. *"Here are the 3 things that actually need you today."* — Priya
5. *"I only check the tool when I remember to."* — Sarah
6. *"I find out in a meeting. That's embarrassing."* — Priya
7. *"It's a framing problem more than a feature problem."* — James

---

## BIGGEST OPEN QUESTION

**Who decides what's urgent — the system or the user?**

James's framing ("blocking question vs. comment") implies urgency must be inferred from context, not just notification type. But the interviews don't reveal whether users would trust an algorithm to make that call, or whether they'd want to configure urgency rules themselves.

If users don't trust the prioritization, they'll ignore the "smart" digest the same way they ignore the current notification list. We need to know: under what conditions would a user act on a notification they didn't seek out themselves?
